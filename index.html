What Survived the ACA Gauntlet

Each bar shows how far a policy component made it — from Obama's 2008 campaign through to February 2026. The gauntlet didn't end when the law was signed. It continued through two Trump administrations and a Biden term. Green bars survived. Yellow bars are eroding. Red bars were eliminated.

The pattern the chart reveals
Repeal failed. Erosion is succeeding. The first Trump administration couldn't repeal the ACA outright (the 2017 "skinny repeal" failed by one vote). Instead, it zeroed out the individual mandate penalty. Biden then expanded the law further than Obama ever had — enhanced subsidies pushed enrollment to a record 22.8 million. But Trump II and the One Big Beautiful Bill Act are now unwinding the coverage gains through three mechanisms: letting enhanced subsidies expire (premiums up 114%, ~4–5 million expected to lose coverage), imposing Medicaid work requirements (estimated 5.2 million to lose coverage by 2034), and proposing to replace subsidies with HSA direct payments and catastrophic plans. The legal architecture of the ACA still stands. But the coverage it provides is contracting.
What the oral history witnesses couldn't have foreseen
The Obama-era witnesses described a law that survived serial near-death experiences through passage and legal challenge. What the chart now shows is a third phase of attrition they didn't anticipate: not repeal, but defunding. The components they fought hardest to include (subsidies, Medicaid expansion) are being hollowed out not by legislation that removes them from the statute books, but by legislation that removes the money that makes them work. Preexisting condition protections — the "quiet survivor" that generated the fewest oral history clips because nobody contested it — remain legally intact. But a protection you can't afford to use is a protection in name only.
The one component that came back from the dead
Drug price negotiation is the only red bar on the chart that turns green again. Obama promised it during the 2008 campaign, then traded it away in the PhRMA deal to secure industry support for the ACA — a move that Sebelius testified left him "very noticeably agitated." The policy was dead for over a decade. Then Biden's Inflation Reduction Act (2022) partially revived it, allowing Medicare to negotiate prices on a limited set of high-cost drugs for the first time. It's the one case where a policy sacrificed for strategic reasons during the Obama era was eventually recovered by a later administration. The oral history witnesses who described the PhRMA deal as a painful but necessary trade couldn't have known the trade would eventually be partially reversed — or that it would take 13 years.
